# Cross-Pipeline Comparison

This bucket compares model performance between the two independent ground-truth pipelines — the 7-step linear aerobiome pipeline and the 10-step, 4-track wetland pipeline. Per-pipeline findings live in [`../summary.md`](../summary.md) (aerobiome) and [`../wetland/summary.md`](../wetland/summary.md); this note interprets what the two evaluations say *together*.

## Figures

- [`cross_pipeline_comparison.png`](../../results/figures/cross_pipeline_comparison.png) — scatter of per-model mean composite score on aerobiome (x) vs wetland (y). The diagonal line represents equal performance across the two pipelines. Points below the diagonal indicate that the model is weaker on wetland than on aerobiome; points above would indicate the reverse (which is empirically absent in the current matrix).
- [`cross_pipeline_gap.png`](../../results/figures/cross_pipeline_gap.png) — horizontal bar chart of the per-model gap (aerobiome mean minus wetland mean), sorted. Positive bars indicate that wetland is harder for that model; near-zero bars indicate a model that transfers its competence. The gap is computed from `results/tables/scoring_matrix.csv` and regenerated by `scripts/generate_cross_pipeline.py`.

## Interpretation

**The diagonal is broken in one direction only.** Every model that scores in the upper half of the aerobiome distribution sits visibly *below* the diagonal on the cross-pipeline scatter. No model currently scores higher on wetland than on aerobiome. This is consistent with the benchmark design: the wetland pipeline adds multi-omics, multi-paradigm, and multi-track complexity on top of the aerobiome toolchain, so any model that clears aerobiome still has three paradigm transitions to pass on wetland.

**Aerobiome competence does not predict wetland competence evenly.** The gap distribution in `cross_pipeline_gap.png` is not uniform. Models that clear aerobiome fully (Claude Opus 4.5, Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3 Pro, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GPT-5) retain relatively strong wetland performance on the shared shotgun-style steps (Steps 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 10) but lose ground specifically at Steps 5, 7, and 8 where the wetland pipeline diverges from the shotgun paradigm. The gap is therefore concentrated in three steps rather than distributed across the benchmark.

**Deep Research interface entries transfer the best.** The three Deep Research variants (ChatGPT, Claude, Google Gemini) show the smallest aerobiome-to-wetland gap. They cannot close the gap entirely — Steps 5, 7, and 8 have zero fully correct entries from *any* family — but they approach the diagonal closely because their retrieval behavior surfaces the niche wetland tools (MEGAN-CE, nanoMDBG + DIAMOND BLASTx NR, OBITools4 + VSEARCH + MIDORI2) that the core-version models cannot produce from parametric memory alone.

**Weak models do not transfer either way.** Entries that fail aerobiome broadly (DeepSeek V3, GLM-5, Gemini 2.0 Flash, older OpenAI entries) also fail wetland broadly. The bottom-left corner of the scatter is populated. The cross-pipeline correlation is visible but the slope is noticeably less than one, which is the operational claim of the dual-pipeline design: pipeline competence does not generalize at a rate of 1:1.

## When to read this bucket

- If you need to know whether a single-pipeline aerobiome result generalizes to a broader multi-omics setting, look at the scatter and the per-model gap.
- If you are evaluating an LLM for a One Health or multi-paradigm bioinformatics workload, the wetland pipeline is the more representative benchmark, and the gap chart tells you which entries retain competence under paradigm switches.
- If you are adapting this benchmark to a new domain, the gap chart is the closest analogue to a "composition penalty" measurement and is the single most useful summary figure for distinguishing retrieval-capable from parametric-only models.
