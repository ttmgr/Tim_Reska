The Problem We Solve

Every AI model in medical underwriting has a blind spot

Current systems produce a risk score for every applicant. A score of "23% high risk" looks the same whether the system had access to 50 data points about that person -- or just 3.

When data is sparse, the AI fills in the blanks with population averages. The output looks confident and reasonable, but it's not based on evidence for that specific person.

We call this "Plausible-but-Wrong": the prediction passes every surface-level check, but carries hidden risk because the input data wasn't good enough to support it.

MedRisk-ADH is the first system that measures input quality before making a prediction and automatically escalates cases where the AI shouldn't be trusted.

How It Works

Three questions before every decision

For each patient, the system asks:

1. How complete is this patient's record?
Are diagnoses, lab results, and medication records present? Or are key pieces missing?

2. Is the data internally consistent?
If someone is coded as diabetic, do their lab values match? If not, something may be wrong with the data.

3. Is the data fresh enough to be useful?
A lab result from 3 years ago tells you much less than one from last month.

These three checks produce a Data Quality Score -- a single number from 0 to 1 that tells the system how much to trust its own input. High score = proceed automatically. Low score = send to a human underwriter.

Why AI-Driven Underwriting

What current methods can't do

Rule-based underwriting uses fixed thresholds with no per-case reliability assessment. Basic ML models impute missing values, which creates the exact plausible-but-wrong predictions we're trying to detect. Neither approach estimates confidence or explains individual decisions.

LLM extension (Phase 3)

Large language models take this further: extracting structured data from unstructured doctor notes across languages (German, French, Spanish, English), generating natural language risk explanations for underwriters, and continuously verifying clinical parameters against new medical literature.

What this means for underwriting operations

Automate the safe cases: When data quality is high, the system's predictions are reliable. These cases can flow through without human review -- faster turnaround, lower cost per policy.

Focus human expertise where it matters: Underwriters spend their time on the cases that actually need judgement -- not reviewing predictions the AI got right anyway.

Catch the hidden risks: Every case where the AI is confident but the data is thin gets flagged automatically. No more silent mispricing.

The bottom line: This isn't a replacement for underwriters. It's a safety layer that makes AI-assisted underwriting defensible -- by knowing when to escalate.
