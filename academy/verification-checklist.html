Verification Checklist

How to catch confident errors before they become your errors.

Claims to Always Check

  • Numbers, dates, names, quotes, laws, policies, prices, deadlines.
  • Causal claims: X caused Y, A is better than B.
  • Anything recent, local, medical, legal, financial, or safety-related.
  • Anything you would be embarrassed to defend without a source.

Three-Layer Check

  • Internal: does the answer contradict itself?
  • Source: do the cited or provided sources actually support the claim?
  • Domain: does it make sense to someone who knows the field?
  • Decision: keep, revise, remove, or escalate.

Fast Source Habits

  • Open the original source, not only a summary.
  • Check date, geography, scope, and definition.
  • Trace numbers back to the table or paragraph they came from.
  • Use two independent sources for important external facts.

Red Flags

  • Overly precise numbers with no source.
  • Citations that are vague, broken, or do not match the claim.
  • A confident answer to a question with missing context.
  • Generic advice where the task needed a specific constraint.

Claim Triage — With One Worked Verification

Risk Example How to Actually Verify
Low Tone, structure, grammar, brainstormed ideas. One human read for fit and clarity. No external check needed.
Medium Market summary, process advice, non-critical analysis. Source check + domain plausibility. Open the cited source, confirm the date and scope, ask one domain-aware colleague.
High AI says: 'EU AI Act Article 4 takes effect August 2026.' (1) Open EUR-Lex 2024/1689; (2) search 'Article 4'; (3) confirm wording: literacy obligation in force Feb 2 2025, transparency obligations from Aug 2 2026; (4) revise the claim to: 'literacy obligations have been in force since 2025-02-02; transparency obligations from 2026-08-02.' Two sources required for client-facing use.
